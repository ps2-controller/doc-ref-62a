Late payments and collections, from the first missed payment

Owner Anurag Angara (PM)
Updated 2026-06-15

Part 1Problem

Where we are today

We service 30,000 mortgage loans. The product onboards loans, runs billing cycles, and accepts payments. The system of record tracks balances and obligations. Borrowers who pay on time have a good experience. Borrowers who fall behind do not. Today the support team tracks past-due loans by hand and reaches out when they can. There are no compliance guardrails, no audit trail, and no tooling beyond what one agent can keep in their head.

What we do not solve today

The product does not act when a borrower misses a payment. It does not remind them. It does not give them a fast path back to current. It does not run a compliant outreach schedule for borrowers who stay delinquent. It cannot produce a record that any of this was attempted. Once a borrower decides to pay, the product handles the payment. Before that, nothing happens.

Why this matters to the business

24.4% of our loans go past the 15-day grace period at least once, and 1.81% reach the 36-day collections trigger. The industry range the New York Fed reports is 0.6% to 1.3%. The 107 loans that are two months behind hold $378K in open balances, 29% of all open dollars in the book.

Three problems follow from this. Higher delinquency raises our servicing cost per loan. It puts investor confidence at risk, because investors look at delinquency rates when picking a servicer. And we are out of compliance: we are required to attempt collections by day 36 and every 7 days after, and we cannot prove we did that for a single loan. Each of these gets worse as the book grows.

Part 2Strategy

Vision for our delinquency offering

In the long run, three things should be true about how we handle a borrower who falls behind.

Borrowers who just need a nudge get one. They hear from us quickly, on a channel they use, with a respectful message and a low-friction path to getting current. At minimum, they make the payment. At best, they leave on the right payment method and on autopay so the miss does not repeat.

Borrowers in genuine hardship get identified early and offered real options. Deferred payments, extended grace windows, temporary rate reductions, term changes. The servicing team is connected to these borrowers quickly, can see the data they need, has a runbook for picking the right option for each case, and has the tooling to act on it.

Borrowers who truly cannot or will not pay have all compliance requirements met. They move into a collections process that reports to credit appropriately and can write off or sell to a collections agency at the correct, compliant time.

We build toward that end state in phases. This PRD is the first phase: the nudge-and-cure loop for the large, cheap-to-fix population, plus a compliant agent path for the hard cases. It sets up the engine and audit trail every later phase needs.

Part 3What we are building

The shape of the solution

The solution has five components. The engine and scheduler are the foundation; everything else plugs into them.

  • A delinquency engine owns the regulatory clock: days past due, what should happen, and when.
  • A scheduler is the loan's timeline: it runs each timed event, either sending a message through a vendor or writing a change back to the servicing core. The same timeline carries loan-modification actions later, so we build it general.
  • Vendor integrations deliver messages over SMS, email, and mail, with a hard consent gate on the digital channels.
  • A repayment portal gives the borrower a pre-filled, one-tap path to cure and turn on autopay.
  • An internal tool gives operations copy management, borrower policy editing, and an agent workspace with a ranked queue.

Future phases (loan modifications, self-serve hardship tools, voice and dialer automation) slot into the same engine and scheduler without a rewrite. v1 also filters the population, so later phases aim at the borrowers who actually need a workout instead of building expensive hardship tooling for people who only needed a text.

What success looks like

The primary metric is the share of delinquent loans that cure before day 36 without an agent call. 95% of past-grace installments that pay today already do so before day 36 with no outreach at all, so the ceiling is real. The secondary metric is the rate of a second consecutive missed payment after a first miss. Catching the first miss should keep borrowers out of the expensive tail.

The leading lever is autopay enablement among borrowers who cure. If that number moves, future delinquency drops on its own. The guardrails are compliance coverage (near 100% of day-36 loans with a logged, on-time attempt) and borrower complaint rate (flat or down, so we know we are not over-messaging).

Section 1Background and context

Where we are today

We service 30,000 mortgage loans. The product onboards a loan with its terms, notification preferences, autopay status, and payment method. It runs billing cycles, tracks due dates and amortization, and accepts payments, including late ones. The system of record tracks balances and obligations, and the on-time experience is good. The payment rail handles late payments too: 24,761 payments in the sample arrived after their due date and were applied correctly, the deepest at 62 days past due.

The past-due experience is where the product falls short. It leans on the support team to track who is behind and on borrowers to use the tools we offer to get current. Our team knows each borrower's preferred channels and can pull up a past-due loan to reach out, but that is the whole of our delinquency capability, and it lives in people's heads. There is no compliant schedule, no audit trail, and no tooling beyond what one agent can track by hand. The product does not act on its own when a borrower misses a payment.

Where we want to head

Servicing is judged on three things at once: collecting for the investor, treating the borrower well, and meeting the rules. Delinquency is where all three are decided, because it is when the borrower is most at risk and the servicer is most visible. Our strategy is to make that moment a product instead of a manual scramble.

We need to build now for two reasons. First, the book is growing, and a manual process that strains at 30,000 loans does not survive at 100,000. Second, the regulatory clock does not wait. We are required to attempt collections by day 36 and every 7 days after, and to charge a late fee once an installment passes the 15-day grace. We can run those rules by hand for a few hundred loans today, but we cannot prove we ran them, and we cannot keep running them by hand as we scale.

The direction is a delinquency experience that reaches every borrower on a channel they use, gives the ones who can pay a path back in seconds, routes the ones who cannot to a prepared agent, and logs every step so we can show a regulator exactly what we did. This document covers the first phase of that build and sets up the rest.

Section 2The problem we are solving

The ideal world

When a borrower falls behind, the path should sort into one of three outcomes.

Borrowers who just need a nudge get one. They hear from us quickly, on a channel they use, with a respectful message and a low-friction path to getting current. At minimum, they make the payment. At best, they leave on the right payment method and on autopay so the miss does not repeat.

Borrowers in genuine hardship get identified early and offered real options. Deferred payments, extended grace windows, temporary rate reductions, term changes. The servicing team is connected to these borrowers quickly, can see the data they need, has a runbook for picking the right option for each case, and has the tooling to act on it.

Borrowers who truly cannot or will not pay have all compliance requirements met. They move into a collections process that reports to credit appropriately and can write off or sell to a collections agency at the correct, compliant time.

What we are solving now, and why this first

This phase builds the timely, compliant, low-friction nudge: catch the miss early and give the borrower a one-tap path to pay and to turn on autopay. We start here for three reasons.

It helps every past-grace borrower, in three different ways. A quarter of the book passes the 15-day grace at least once, and the borrowers who do split into three groups. Most pay back within a few weeks; a smaller group drags on past day 36; a small tail never pays at all. A reminder and a cure page is the same cheap tool for all three. For the first group, it heads off a late fee they would otherwise pay. For the second, it pulls them onto autopay so the next miss does not happen, which matters because off-autopay loans pass grace at 41% against 1.6% on. For the third, it is the start of the compliance trail the regulatory clock requires.

It builds the foundation everything else needs. The engine, scheduler, and audit trail are not throwaway pieces for a notification feature. Later phases will give operators more options for borrowers in genuine hardship, and the same scheduler that sends a payment reminder today is what later fires a due-date change and the confirmation message that goes with it. Building these now means later phases plug in instead of forcing a rewrite.

It clears the noise so the next problem is easier to see. Once the borrowers who just needed a nudge have self-cured, the ones left are the ones who need a workout. That makes Phase 2 cheaper to design and run, because hardship tooling aims at borrowers who actually need it.

What we are not solving now, and how we get there

The vision in Section 2 has three borrower outcomes: a nudge for the forgetful, real workout options for the genuinely stuck, and a compliant collections path for the ones who cannot or will not pay. This phase covers the first one and the regulatory floor of the third one. The middle outcome waits for Phase 2.

The forgetful borrower is covered now. A reminder, a one-tap cure, and an autopay prompt. That is the bulk of the volume and the cheapest fix per loan.

The stuck borrower is reached now, but not yet served. The day-36 compliance attempt happens, an agent picks up the call, and the reason for delinquency is recorded. But this phase does not give that borrower a workout. Deferred payments, term changes, rate reductions, and the rest of loss mitigation are Phase 2. We need to talk to the borrower before we can design what to offer them, and we need to clear the forgetful group out of the queue so the work that remains is genuine hardship.

The unrecoverable borrower is out of scope. Credit reporting, charge-off, and sale to a collections agency follow the workout phase, because they are the path for borrowers we have already tried to help and could not.

Two other things are deliberately left out: voice and dialer automation, because the day-36 group needs a person and voice can be added later as one more channel; and granular roles and permissions for operations, because the audit log tied to SSO identity is enough accountability for the team size we have today.

What we already do, so scope is clear

We already accept late payments and recalculate the balance when one lands. The 24,761 late payments in the sample prove the rail is in place. The gap is not taking a late payment. The gap is having no experience that prevents the miss, resolves it fast when it happens, and stays compliant throughout.

Why a product, not better borrower behavior

Our current product gives a borrower the option to pay and the option to set up autopay. It does not act when they do neither, and the 41% past-grace rate on autopay-off loans is what leaving it to the borrower produces. Offering options is not the same as running a process. A servicer's job in delinquency is to run the process: remind on a schedule, apply fees by the rule, attempt collections by the deadline, and give the borrower a frictionless way back. None of that happens on its own. We offer the tools to make late payments, but no dedicated experience to prevent or collect on them, which is one of the main reasons our delinquency rate is far above the industry standard.

Section 3User impact

There are two borrower populations and one internal team. Each carries a different business cost. Full segment sizes and rules are in Appendix A.

Borrower Segments

We segment borrowers on two behaviors that predict most repayment behavior: whether a loan is on autopay, and how deep its delinquency runs. Autopay is the strongest single predictor. A loan on autopay passes the 15-day grace 1.6% of the time; a loan off autopay, 41%. Every one of the 542 loans that reached collections was off autopay. Depth then separates the borrower who slips once and recovers from the one who is stuck. Those two cuts produce the two populations below.

PopulationSizeBehaviorWhat it needs

The forgetful borrower (primary)

This is where the volume and the cheapest wins are. The forgetful borrower can pay and intends to pay. They missed a due date because life got in the way, autopay was never set up, or a card expired. In the data, they are the 6,583 loans that pass grace and cure on their own, plus most of the wider 7,331 past-grace group. They do not need help. They need a reminder and a path that takes ten seconds.

Today, their experience is silence, then a surprise late fee. The current product gives them the option to pay and walk into the portal on their own, and 41% of off-autopay loans pass grace anyway. The option is not enough. After this phase: a reminder on a channel they chose, a clear statement of what is owed, and a pre-filled page where they pay in one tap and are asked whether they want autopay on so it does not happen again.

Value to the business. They move the headline delinquency number at the lowest cost per loan. The autopay ask at the cure moment is the strongest lever we have against future misses. If their behavior shifts, the whole funnel narrows, which keeps servicing cost per loan flat as the book grows.

The stuck borrower (secondary: route, do not solve)

The 542 loans that reach day 36 are small in count but heavy in dollars and compliance exposure. Inside them, 107 are two full months behind and hold $378K, 29% of all open dollars. Some face real hardship, some are unreachable, some are both. A reminder will not fix a borrower two months behind.

Today there is no schedule that guarantees they are reached and no record that they were. The current process leans on an agent remembering to follow up, which does not survive scale and cannot be proven to a regulator. After this phase: a compliant outreach cadence that does not let them fall through a crack and, when reached, a prepared agent who states the amount, seeks a Promise-to-Pay, and records whether the hardship is temporary or permanent.

Value to the business. They are the regulatory requirement and the dollar concentration. We must attempt collections by day 36 and every 7 days after, and prove it, which today we cannot do for a single loan. We capture what they need now so Phase 2 can serve the workout. We are not building the workout itself in this phase.

The servicing agent (internal)

The agent is the person who works the stuck-borrower tail. Today they have no queue, no script, and no record of what was said. Compliance rests on individual memory, and the work does not scale past a handful of agents. After this phase they get a worklist ranked by days past due and balance, a script that walks the required disclosures and the Promise-to-Pay ask, and a one-click way to log the call and capture the reason for delinquency. They can also edit borrower policy (autopay, payment method) and seed a one-off communication. Each action is logged with who acted and an optional reason.

Value to the business. The stuck-borrower group cannot be automated away. Only 24% consent to a digital channel and the rest need phone and mail. Equipping the agent makes the compliance requirement achievable as the book grows, instead of a cost that scales with delinquent loans.

Section 4The shape of the solution

The solution is five parts. The engine and the timeline are the core that everything else plugs into, and they lean on the servicing core we already run. In one line each:

  • Delinquency engine. Watches each loan and decides what should happen and when: a late fee here, a reminder there, a collections attempt by day 36.
  • Scheduler, the timeline. Holds those decisions as timed events and runs each one on its day, either sending a message or writing a change back to the servicing core.
  • Vendor integrations. The connection to SMS, email, and mail, with a consent check before any digital message goes out.
  • Internal tool. Where operations sets the message copy, edits a borrower's autopay or payment method, and works the agent queue. Every action is logged.
  • Repayment portal. The page a nudge links to, where the borrower pays in one tap and can turn on autopay.

The map below shows how they connect. Each part then gets its own section: what it does now, what it sets up for later, how we know it worked, and the decisions behind it.

1. Delinquency engine

The engine owns the regulatory clock. It decides what should happen on a loan and when, then writes timed events to the scheduler. It does not send messages or change loan state directly.

Functionality

  • Compute days past due for every loan, every day a case is open, against the installment due date.
  • Open a delinquency case when the servicing core reports an unpaid installment, and close it when a qualifying payment posts.
  • Write timed events to the scheduler for the lifecycle: grace reminders, late fee, day-36 attempt, and 7-day repeat attempts.
  • Pause and resume the schedule based on payment, Promise-to-Pay, and broken-promise events.
  • Expose a read/write API: the servicing core posts payment and policy events into it; internal tooling reads case state and seeds agent-initiated actions.

Business rules

  • Grace window. No outreach or fee before day 16. The 15-day grace is fixed policy.
  • Late fee. Applied once, the first time days past due exceeds 15 for an installment. Never twice for the same installment, even on reprocess.
  • First collections attempt. Fires no later than day 36 from the installment due date, with margin so processing delays do not push it past the deadline.
  • Repeat attempts. Every 7 days after the last attempt while the case is open. The cadence never silently lapses.
  • Pause conditions. A qualifying payment closes the case. A recorded Promise-to-Pay pauses attempts until the promised date. If the promise date passes unpaid, the cadence resumes.
  • Idempotency. A late payment that lands before the engine opens the case reconciles cleanly: if the servicing core has already applied it, no case opens.
  • Snapshot at open. The opening event records the loan, installment, due date, amount due, and the borrower's notification preferences, payment method, and autopay status at that moment.

2. Scheduler (the timeline)

The scheduler is the loan's timeline. It holds every timed event and runs each one when its day arrives. An event resolves one of two ways: it tells a vendor to send a message, or it writes a change back to the servicing core. The same timeline that fires a day-36 reminder today will later post a due-date extension to the servicing core and send the letter that goes with it.

Functionality

  • Hold the full set of planned events for each open case, with their fire dates and the channel rules to apply at fire time.
  • Run each event on its day and route it to its sink: a vendor connector for an outreach event, or the servicing core for a state-change event (late fee today, autopay and loan modifications later).
  • Pick the channel for an outreach event at fire time, against the borrower's current consent and the channel-ranking rule.
  • Insert, reschedule, and cancel events on a loan, whether the source is the engine, an agent action, or a future loan-modification action.
  • Write an audit record for every scheduled and fired event: what was planned, what fired, on which channel, what the vendor returned, and the borrower policy snapshot at that moment.
  • Expose a queryable schedule for one loan so the internal tool can show an agent what is planned and when.

Business rules

  • Channel selection. Pick the top-ranked consented channel at fire time. A preference change before the event still routes correctly, because the channel is not frozen at plan time.
  • Channel ranking. Driven by what the borrower consents to and what the data says drives cure. Operations can adjust the ranking; the engine cannot bypass consent.
  • Delivery fallback. If the vendor reports a failed send, the scheduler falls back to the next eligible channel rather than going silent.
  • Honor opt-outs immediately. An opt-out posted between plan time and fire time removes the channel from eligibility for that event and future events.
  • Audit completeness. No event fires without producing an audit record. A missing delivery status is recorded as missing, not omitted.
  • Copy pulled at send time. The wording for an outreach event is fetched from copy management when the event fires, so an operations update applies to the next send without a deploy.

3. Vendor integrations

Vendor integrations are the connectors for the three channels in this phase: SMS, email, and mail. Each connector accepts a send request from the scheduler, delivers it, and reports status back. Voice is a likely future channel and the integration layer is designed to take it without rework.

Functionality

  • Accept a send request from the scheduler with the resolved channel, recipient, and copy.
  • Deliver the message through the vendor.
  • Return delivery status (queued, sent, delivered, failed, bounced) so the scheduler can write a complete audit record.
  • Surface vendor errors in a normalized form so a failed send can fall back to the next channel.

Business rules

  • Consent gate. SMS and email are sent only to borrowers who have consented to that channel. The gate is a hard constraint in the integration layer, not a config a campaign can override.
  • Phone and mail. Always permitted under the regulatory rules. Phone calls happen through the agent workspace, not the connector.
  • Send-time check. Consent is checked at send time against current preferences, not at plan time.
  • Opt-out propagation. An opt-out received from a vendor (an SMS STOP, an email unsubscribe) updates the borrower's preferences and removes the channel from future eligibility.
  • Status reporting. No send is considered complete until the connector returns a final status. Missing status is recorded as such and surfaced to operations.

4. Internal tool (operations)

The internal tool has three capabilities. Every change to any of them carries an audit log: timestamp, who acted (via SSO), and an optional reason. Granular roles and permissions are out of scope for this phase. Every action is tied to an SSO identity, which is enough to start.

Functionality

  • No-code copy management. Operations sets the copy for each message permutation and filters by the conditions that trigger it (channel, day-30 message, late-fee notice, and so on). The first version sets copy. A later version adds variant experiments to find what drives cure rate per message and channel.
  • Borrower policy updates. Operations can edit autopay status and payment method on a borrower's behalf, for example when a borrower asks to turn autopay on during a call.
  • Agent collections workspace. A worklist ranked by days past due and balance, seeded from the day-36 group. A script prompts the agent through the required disclosures and the Promise-to-Pay ask. The agent can log a contact, capture a reason for delinquency, and seed a follow-up event on the scheduler.

Business rules

  • Copy changes apply to the next send. No deploy is required and no backfill rewrites past sends. Audit records of past sends keep the copy as it was at fire time.
  • Policy edits are logged with actor and reason. Autopay and payment-method changes flow through the same writeback path the borrower uses, so the servicing core sees one source of truth.
  • Worklist ranking. Days past due first, then balance. The worklist refreshes as cases pause, close, or advance.
  • Agent script captures required fields. The total amount due, a Promise-to-Pay date if offered, the reason for delinquency, and whether the hardship is temporary or permanent. Calls cannot be logged without these fields.
  • Manual outreach. A seeded message routes through the scheduler and the consent gate like any other event. Agents cannot bypass channel rules.
  • SSO accountability. Every change is tied to the actor's SSO identity. Operations acts under their own login.

5. Low-friction repayment portal

The repayment portal is the borrower-facing destination for every nudge. A nudge does not drop the borrower on a generic dashboard. It links to a page scoped to the exact action the message is about, pre-filled.

Functionality

  • Render a cure page scoped to a single loan and a single past-due balance, pre-filled with the amount and the borrower's payment method.
  • Offer one primary action (pay the past-due amount) and one secondary action (turn on autopay), with nothing else competing for the tap.
  • Post a payment to the servicing core, which notifies the engine, which closes the case and cancels the rest of the schedule.
  • Persist an autopay enablement made from the page so the next billing cycle uses it.

Business rules

  • Pre-filled amount. The page shows the current past-due amount from the servicing core, including any late fee. If the balance has changed since the nudge fired, the page reflects the current balance.
  • Pre-filled method. The default payment method is the one on file. The borrower can change it on the page.
  • Autopay prompt. Shown by default on the cure page for any borrower not currently on autopay. Hidden when autopay is already on. The prompt explains that autopay takes effect on the next due date.
  • One-tap path. A successful payment closes the case in one round trip. The portal does not require a separate login flow from a deep link.
  • Deep-link scope. Each link is scoped to a single loan and installment. A link cannot be reused to act on a different loan.
  • Failure handling. A failed payment leaves the case open and the schedule untouched. The borrower sees a clear error and can retry.

How the pieces fire, end to end

The delinquency workflow, stage by stage

The diagram above is the path; this is the operating spec. Pick a stage to see what the tooling does at that moment and the requirements behind it. Each requirement group expands, and each group names the component that owns it, so this doubles as the build checklist for the five components. The day numbers are the engine's clock: grace ends at day 15, the late fee lands the day after, and the first collections attempt is due by day 36.

Trigger: a scheduled installment is not paid by its due date

The servicing core tells the engine the installment came due unpaid. The engine opens a delinquency case on the loan, starts the days-past-due count from the due date, and writes the opening event to the audit trail. Nothing is sent to the borrower yet.

Detect the miss and open a caseDelinquency engine
  • On a payment.due_unpaid event from the servicing core, the engine opens a case keyed to the loan and the installment within one processing cycle.
  • Days past due is computed as calendar days from the due date, recomputed each day the case is open, so a regulator reading the record sees the same number the rules act on.
  • A late payment that lands before the case opens reconciles cleanly: if the servicing core has already applied the payment, the engine opens no case.
  • The opening event records the loan, installment, due date, amount due, and the borrower's notification preferences, payment method, and autopay status at that moment.
Set the schedule for this caseScheduler
  • When the engine opens the case it asks the scheduler to lay down the planned events for the lifecycle: the grace reminders, the day-16 late fee and notice, and the day-36 attempt.
  • Each planned event carries its fire date and the channel rule to apply when it fires, not a frozen channel, so a preference change before the event still routes correctly.
  • The schedule is queryable for one loan, so the internal tool can show an agent exactly what is planned and when.
Compliance gate

No borrower contact in this stage. The only obligation is an accurate, timestamped record that the case opened and the clock started.

Exit conditions

Payment posts and the engine closes the case, or the clock advances into the grace window.

Days 1 to 15 past due, inside grace, no late fee yet

The engine schedules light, respectful reminders on the borrower's consented channels. The aim is the ten-second cure: a borrower who simply forgot pays from the nudge, and most do, well before any fee applies.

Pick the channel and send the reminderScheduler + vendors
  • The scheduler ranks the borrower's consented channels by what the data says drives a cure, and sends on the top eligible one.
  • SMS and email go only to borrowers who have consented to that channel. Mail is always permitted. The check happens at send time against current preferences.
  • The vendor connector returns a delivery status, and the scheduler writes it to the case, so a failed send can fall back to the next channel rather than going silent.
  • Copy is whatever operations has set for this message type and channel, pulled at send time so a wording change needs no deploy.
Land the borrower on a one-action pageRepayment portal
  • Every reminder links to a page scoped to this loan and this balance, with the amount and payment method pre-filled.
  • The page offers one primary action, pay the past-due amount, and one secondary, turn on autopay, with nothing else competing for the tap.
  • A payment on the page posts to the servicing core, which notifies the engine, which closes the case and cancels the rest of the schedule.
Compliance gate

Send only on consented channels. Honor any opt-out immediately and record it. No late fee may be charged before day 16.

Exit conditions

Borrower pays and the case closes, or day 15 passes without payment and the case enters the late-fee stage.

Day 16: the installment passes the 15-day grace

The engine applies the late fee and schedules a clear notice that names the fee and the new amount due. The tone of outreach steps up from a gentle reminder to a direct, still respectful, prompt.

Apply the late fee exactly onceDelinquency engine
  • The fee is applied the first time days past due exceeds 15, and never twice for the same installment, even if the engine reprocesses the case.
  • The fee posts through the servicing core so the borrower's balance and the portal show the same number.
  • The fee event is timestamped and tied to the rule that triggered it, so the charge is reproducible from the record.
Notify the borrower of the fee and the path backScheduler + vendors
  • The notice states the fee, the updated total due, and a link to the cure page, in the copy operations has set for the late-fee message.
  • It routes by the same consent and ranking rules, so a borrower who only allows mail gets a mailed notice and the fee is not the first they hear of it on a channel they never approved.
  • The send and its delivery status are written to the case.
Compliance gate

The fee follows the disclosed grace rule and is charged once. The borrower is told the fee was applied through a channel they consented to.

Exit conditions

Borrower pays the past-due amount and fee and the case closes, or the clock continues toward day 36.

Day 36: the regulatory first-attempt deadline

The engine fires the first required collections attempt and adds the loan to the agent worklist. This is the handoff from automated nudging to a person, and it is the moment the small stuck tail, the 542 loans, becomes work a human owns.

Fire the attempt by the deadlineDelinquency engine + scheduler
  • The first attempt is scheduled to fire no later than day 36, measured from the due date, with margin so a processing delay never pushes it past the deadline.
  • The attempt and its timestamp are recorded as the compliance artifact that proves the deadline was met.
  • If the borrower already made a Promise-to-Pay or a qualifying payment, the schedule is paused and the attempt does not fire.
Build the agent worklist and scriptInternal tool (agent workspace)
  • The loan enters a worklist ranked by days past due and balance, so the deepest and largest cases surface first.
  • The agent view shows the loan state, the contact history with channels and delivery status, and the amount due, all from the case record.
  • A script prompts the agent to state the total due, seek a Promise-to-Pay, and record the reason for delinquency and whether the hardship is temporary or permanent.
  • The agent can log the contact outcome and seed a follow-up, which the scheduler picks up as a new event on the loan.
Compliance gate

An attempt is made and recorded by day 36. Every contact carries the channel, time, and actor, so the first-attempt obligation is provable.

Exit conditions

Promise-to-Pay or payment pauses the case, or it advances to repeat attempts every 7 days.

After day 36: a required attempt every 7 days until resolved

The engine schedules a repeat attempt every 7 days and keeps the case live until the past-due amount is paid or a Promise-to-Pay holds it. A kept promise pauses the cadence; a broken one resumes it.

Hold the 7-day cadenceDelinquency engine + scheduler
  • Each attempt is scheduled 7 days after the last while the case is open, so the cadence is continuous and provable.
  • Every attempt is recorded with its channel and time, building the run of evidence a regulator expects across a long delinquency.
  • The cadence runs until a pause condition is met, never silently lapsing.
Honor a Promise-to-PayDelinquency engine + agent workspace
  • An agent records a Promise-to-Pay with a date, and the engine pauses attempts until that date.
  • If the promised payment arrives, the case closes. If the date passes unpaid, the cadence resumes automatically.
  • The promise, its date, and the agent who recorded it are in the audit trail.
Compliance gate

Attempts continue on the required cadence and stay on consented channels. A Promise-to-Pay is the only thing that pauses outreach, and it is logged.

Exit conditions

Payment resolves and closes the case, or both installments go unpaid and the loan routes to the workout group for Phase 2.

Resolution: the case closes, or the loan routes onward

A case ends one of two ways. Most end in a cure: the borrower pays, and ideally turns on autopay so it does not happen again. A few route to the hardship path, where Phase 2 loss-mitigation tools take over. Either way the loan leaves the automated cadence with a complete record.

Close on a cure and prevent the next missDelinquency engine + portal
  • On a qualifying payment the engine closes the case, cancels remaining scheduled events, and stops any late-fee accrual for that installment.
  • If the borrower turns on autopay from the cure page, operations or the portal records the change, and the data backs why this matters: autopay loans almost never pass grace.
  • The close event is recorded so the loan's path from miss to cure is reproducible end to end.
Route a stuck loan to the workout pathInternal tool + scheduler
  • A loan the agent marks as genuine hardship is tagged for the workout group, the 107 two-months-behind loans being the clearest case.
  • In this phase the route is a clean handoff with the reason and history attached. The loss-mitigation actions themselves, deferral and term changes, are Phase 2.
  • The handoff preserves the full case record, so the next step starts from the same facts rather than a cold file.
Compliance gate

The closed or routed state is recorded with its reason and timestamp, so the loan's full timeline can be reconstructed on demand.

Section 5Rollout

The rollout does two things in order. First, prove the rules before any borrower is touched. Then expand toward the full delinquency experience one validated step at a time. The phasing is how we build toward Section 2: every borrower is reached, the self-curing top handles itself, and the stuck tail is worked by a person with a record behind every contact.

What ships first, and why

Two questions decide the order: does it unblock compliance, and does it move the primary metric. The digital self-cure loop and the engine answer both, so they go first. The agent and mail path follows for the hard cases. Operations controls come last, once there is a working loop to tune.

PriorityCapabilityWhy it ranks here
P0Delinquency engine + scheduler + auditNothing is compliant or measurable without this foundation. It is also the hardest to retrofit.
P0SMS and email integration with consent gateThe cheapest channels reach 73% of the book and cover most of the self-curing top.
P0Repayment portal with one-tap pay and autopay toggleThis is the conversion surface for the primary metric. A nudge with no easy action is wasted.
P0Late-fee application on the 15-day ruleA direct regulatory requirement, and simple given the engine.
P1Mail integrationRequired to reach the day-36 group, 80% of whom take mail, but slower and less urgent than digital for the wide top.
P1Agent collections workspace + Promise-to-Pay captureNeeded for the day-36 compliance attempts and the human side of the rule. Smaller population, so it can follow the digital top by a beat.

Testing

  • Unit and scenario tests on the engine's clock: grace boundary at day 15, first attempt by day 36, the 7-day cadence, and correct pause on payment and on Promise-to-Pay. These are the rules a regulator would check, so they get the most coverage.
  • A compliance dry run: replay the current book through the engine in a non-sending mode and confirm every loan that reached day 36 would have had a logged, on-time attempt. This both validates the engine and produces the baseline.
  • Channel-gate tests proving we never send SMS or email without consent.
  • Portal usability testing on the cure-and-autopay flow, because taps lost here are cures lost.

The staged sequence

A staged rollout that earns trust before it scales. Each step builds on the last, and the order walks straight from no borrower contact to the full experience.

  1. Shadow mode. The engine runs and logs what it would do, sends nothing. We compare its decisions against the regulatory rules and fix gaps with no borrower impact.
  2. Small live cohort. Turn on real sends for a few percent of the past-grace population, digital channels only. Watch cure rate, opt-outs, and complaints.
  3. Expand the digital top. Roll out to the full self-curing population as the cure metric holds and guardrails stay flat.
  4. Turn on the agent path. Bring the day-36 workspace and mail outreach online for the hard cases once the digital loop is proven.
  5. Hand copy to operations. Open no-code copy management, then later the experimentation layer.

Measuring

Instrument from shadow mode so the baseline is honest. The primary readout is the cure-before-day-36 rate against that baseline, with the second-miss rate close behind. Autopay enablement is the leading indicator we expect to move first. The guardrails (compliance coverage, opt-out and complaint rate) gate every expansion step: if compliance coverage is not at or near 100%, or complaints spike, we hold.

What each team needs to know to execute

A short brief per function so eng, design, and operations can run with this.

Engineering. The engine and scheduler are the foundation and the hard part, so build them general and auditable, not as a reminder cron. The any-installment rollup and the day-count rules in the data layer are the source of truth for delinquency state; keep one definition. The consent gate is a hard constraint in the integration layer, not a config a campaign can override. Every scheduled event must snapshot borrower preferences, payment method, and autopay status at fire time so success is measurable.

Design. The cure page is the conversion surface for the primary metric, so optimize for one screen and one decision, with the autopay prompt in context rather than buried. Outreach copy and cadence must read as respectful, because the guardrail is borrower trust. The agent workspace is a real product too: a ranked worklist and a script that makes the required disclosures impossible to skip.

Operations. You own the metric once copy is in your hands. Plan for tuning copy by message and channel against cure rate. The agent script is not boilerplate, it encodes the regulatory call requirements, so changes to it run through compliance. Until granular roles exist, the audit log plus SSO identity is how we stay accountable, so act under your own login.

Where the rollout leads: Phase 3 and the full vision

Phases 1 and 2 stop short of the Section 2 vision in one specific way: when a borrower cannot self-cure, we have a prepared agent and a compliant trail, but we do not yet have the workout actions the agent needs to actually resolve the case. Phase 3 closes that gap. By then Phases 1 and 2 have filtered out the borrowers who only needed a nudge, so the workout tools aim at the small, dollar-heavy tail that actually needs them. The same engine, scheduler, and audit trail carry every new action, so this is capability added on top, not a second system.

Phase 3 has four pieces, in rough order:

  • Loan modifications on the same scheduler. The workout actions the agent path leads to: defer a payment, extend a due date, a temporary rate reduction, a term change. Each writes back to the servicing core through the same timeline that fires today's late-fee event, and each is bundled with the compliant communication that explains the new terms. A loan's state stays reproducible from its timeline.
  • Hardship identification and self-serve options. With the noise filtered out, we can spot genuine hardship earlier and surface a subset of the workout actions to the borrower directly, with the agent path as the fallback for cases that need a conversation.
  • Voice as a scheduled channel. 76% of the stuck group has no digital channel, so phone is the only way to reach them at scale. Phase 3 makes outbound voice a scheduled, audited event through the integration layer, alongside SMS, email, and mail, so the cadence and the record stay uniform across channels.
  • Operations runs the loop. No-code copy management and an experimentation layer so operations can tune outreach by message and channel against cure rate without an engineering ticket. Granular roles and permissions land here too, once the action surface is wide enough to justify them.

At the end of Phase 3, the three borrower outcomes from the Section 2 vision are all served by one product: a nudge for the borrower who just needs reminding, a real workout for the borrower in hardship, and a compliant collections process for the borrower who will not pay. Operations owns the metric and the copy, engineering maintains the engine, and every action is on the same audited timeline.

Section 6The ask

This document asks for alignment on four things, so we can start building the first phase.

  1. The problem. Our delinquency rate runs above industry norms and gets more expensive as the book grows, we have no first-class experience for borrowers who fall behind, and we cannot prove a single compliant collections attempt today. Offering borrowers the option to pay has not closed the gap, and it will not.
  2. Who we solve for, and why this first. The forgetful borrower, who can pay and just needs a timely, low-friction nudge. They are the largest, cheapest win, and serving them clears the noise so we can see who genuinely needs a workout.
  3. The shape of the solution. A delinquency engine and scheduler with full audit history, digital outreach with a consent gate, a one-tap cure-and-autopay portal, and an agent workspace for the day-36 tail.
  4. The key decisions and the long-term vision. Build a general engine rather than a reminder job, push autopay at the cure moment, route the hard cases to people, and audit everything from day one, all chosen so the later phases of the delinquency experience plug into the same foundation.

Open questions to settle as we build

A few decisions are deliberately left open here, because they are better made with the team and with early data.

  • What is the right first-touch timing inside the grace window? The data shows two thirds of late payers cure within 15 days, so a gentle pre-grace reminder may lift cure rate, but it risks over-messaging. Test it.
  • Do we apply the late fee silently or pair it with the notice? Pairing is more transparent and likely better for trust, and it is the default assumption here.
  • What is the Promise-to-Pay policy when a borrower commits but misses the commitment? The engine needs a defined resume behavior, which is a compliance and operations decision.
  • How hard do we push autopay for borrowers who repeatedly opt out? There is a respect-versus-effectiveness line here worth setting deliberately.

AssumptionsWhat this PRD takes as given

A short list of what the plan assumes, and what each one changes about the PRD. If one is wrong, the part that leans on it needs a second look.

  • The product already onboards borrowers, runs billing, and accepts payments, including late ones. Impact: this phase adds the delinquency experience on top, it does not rebuild servicing.
  • The product keeps no audit history today. Impact: the compliance audit trail is built from scratch here, not bolted onto an existing log.
  • Autopay takes effect on the next due date and pays the remaining minimum. Impact: the cure flow can safely ask a borrower to turn it on and count on it for the next cycle.
  • The regulatory clock is fixed: late fee after the 15-day grace, first collections attempt by day 36, a repeat every 7 days. Impact: the engine treats these as policy, and compliance coverage is measured against them.
  • The two-month sample represents the live book. Impact: every headline number, including the 24% digital-consent split that sends the day-36 group to a person, is taken as current and confirmed over a longer window before targets are set.
  • Catching the first miss early prevents some borrowers from reaching the stuck tail. Impact: this is the bet behind the prevention metric, and the shadow period exists to test it.
  • Loan modifications, hardship detection, voice, formal roles, and loan sales are out of scope for now. Impact: the engine and integrations are built to carry them later, but the plan does not depend on them.
